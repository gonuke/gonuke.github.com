Don't Miss These Sights


We spent the equivalent of a few days exploring the varied and famous sights of Florence. As with most other places in Italy, there is a church around every corner. In this case there are many fabulous and elaborate churches in addition to the Duomo (cathedral) which is an unmissable landmark in the middle of the city. My favourite, and the first that we visited is the Basilica Santa Croce. It is completely full with tombs and monuments to a long list of famous people from the Renaissance on. In the classical realm, you can find Dante (this author of the Divine Comedy is a favourite celebrity of Florence), Ghiberti (most well known for his brass bas-relief doors on the Duomo's Baptistry), Machiavelli (whose ideas of ruling and governing were revolutionary in his book The Price), Rossini (who wrote the Barber of Seville - see Bugs Bunny) and one of Florence's favourite sons, Michelangelo. In the realm of science, the list begins with Galileo and ends with a personal favourite, Enrico Firmi, the designer of the very first man-made nuclear reactor. There was also a monument to Marcone, the inventor of wireless radio. Of course, other than all the dead people who are celebrated there, the church itself is a magnificent piece of architecture filled with marvelous frescoes and stained glass.

On the theme of Churches, there is also a magnificent Synagogue in Florence, although it is only about 150 years old. It was built when the emancipation of the Jews of Florence was combined with the coincidental death of one of their richest who lef all his money for the construction of the building. It was is a monumental building which makes its mark on the skyline of Florence, equalling most of the best churches. Although it is fairly recent built, it was intentionally designed to reflect the origins of the Jewish population in Florence. In addition to the various traditional styles of the Jews from Spain and Portugal, the most noticable feature is the unending decoration of the walls following a moorish superstition that empty spaces would bring bad luck.

The discussion of churches would not be complete without the Duomo of Florence. This building completely dominates the city with its size and location. Directly in the middle of Florence this garganutuan cathedral is absolutely full of dead people! Nearly every square inch of the floor is covered with some kind of tombstone for some person whose name I didn't recognize. The dome of the cathedral is the highest feature of Florence and you can climb up the inside of it get a good view of Florence. We didn't spend a lot of time here, however, because of all the other tourists - more on that later.

Another major feature of Florence, not to be missed, is the Galleria dell' Accademia. This is none other than the home of Michelanglo's David in full glory. David is the true centerpoint of this gallery at the end of the first and main hallway in a small dome all to himself. Other attractions of the Accademia are many plaster models from some of the old professors of the accademy used before making marble final statues and more "Adoration of Christ"s, "Annunciation"s and "Martyrdom of Saint ...."s than you can shake a stick at! The best thing about all these paintings on a similar theme is that they have very interesting and informative explanations which help explain a lot about the history of the area and the history of art in the Renaissance.

The other important gallery is perhaps the most famous: the Uffizi. This magnificent art gallery was bombed in 1993 by the Mafia in an attempt to slow down the government prossecutions. Unfortunately they are still recovering, at least in part, from this explosion which destroyed much of the west wing of the gallery which houses the most recent works. According to one of my colleagues, when he was there a couple of year ago, they had moved most of the paintings from this wing to the other wing so that they could be seen. I think they must have finished the renovations and were in the process of returning them all to their original places, hence the closings. My favourite part of this gallery was the Botticelli room where I was a stowaway on a very long, informative description of Bottecelli's "Allegory of Spring". Most of you have probably seen this painting somewhere in a book, on a greeting card, or the like. The provider of all this great information was an art history student from Dartmouth who spoke for well over 45 minutes about all the imagery, history and important artistic aspects of this painting.

Not all the sights of Florence are buildings or their contents. The Gardens of Boboli are a wonderful place to spend a morning, an afternoon, or even a whole day. At the top of the gardens, in addition to a small porcelain museum and Fort Belvedere, there is a wonderful view back towards the center of the city. There are also a few little things hidden around the city which are best found by accident.
