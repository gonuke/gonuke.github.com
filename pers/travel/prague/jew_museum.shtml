The Jewish Museum of Prague

> Jewish Museum

Finally, before leaving on Sunday morning, we toured the Jewish Museum, distributed amongst 4 old synagogues and the cemetery in the old Jewish neighborhood. One site (The Klaus Synagogue) is about Judaism itself, describing various aspects of the religion using various antiques from the Prague neighborhoods while another site (The Maisel Synagogue) describes the history of the Jewish people in the Czech Republic, and more specifically, within Prague. These, however, are not the most interesting parts! The Pinkas Synagogue has been emptied completely and the walls have been painted with the names of every single Czech Jewish person who went missing during the Second World War. Ordered first by the last known town of residence and then by last name, both floors of the synagogue nearly full (the top floor has room for any that have, for whatever reason, been missed). Unfortunately, I did not know enough about the my grandfather's first family, whose names must certainly be there, to find them in the lists. The Old Cemetery is a tightly packed collection of gravestones, not much bigger than most people's backyards. The graveyard is so small and packed that it looks like a forest of stones. Finally, the "Old-New Synagogue" is the oldest of all the synagogues and was obviously intended only for the men: not only it is very small, with a single row of seats around the outside, there is absolutely no gallery, room, or separated area for women.
