Best Irish Walks

2nd Edition
Edited by Joss Lynam

(Gill & Macmillan, Dublin, 1996)

> Best Irish Walks

This is a perfect book for anyone wanting to see the hills of Ireland from a quiet and solitary perspective. Seventy-six different walks are given for a wide range of walking enthusiasts. There are one-way walks and circular walks, steep walks and flat walks, long walks and short walks, and combinations of all of these. Although spread throughout Ireland, most are concentrated in the popular areas well known to tourists to Ireland: the Wicklow Hills south of Dublin, Co. Kerry, Connemara, and Co. Donegal.

Every walk is accompanied by a fairly detailed map, based in most cases on the ordinance survey map for that region. The route is clearly indicated on the map and described in detail in the text. Also, for each walk, the total distance, total ascent, and total walking time is indicated. The walking time is based on relatively moderate speeds, and should be representative for most casual walkers. One of the most interesting features of this book, however, is the background which it gives for the walks. In many cases, woven throughout the description of the route of the walk is some combination of the political, natural and geological history.

Although we planned to do more, the low clouds during much of our trip meant that we only managed two of these walks. We had planned to walk over Purple Mountain (#30), but instead spent the day on horseback. Our guide that day, Gene Tangney, told us that the Purple Mountain was perhaps the most beautiful walk in Ireland. He also appeased us, confirming that with the low clouds, it would not have been worth it. We did manage to walk the Cliffs of Moher (#44), which is absolutely breathtaking, if not a little treacherous. Our most beautiful day was spent climbing the three connected peaks of Damph, Slieve Main, and Slieve Snaght on Inishown in Co. Donegal (#68).
